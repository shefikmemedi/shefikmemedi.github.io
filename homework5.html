Homework 5 – Measures of Location and Dispersion

Introduction

Statistical analysis begins by understanding two core dimensions: location (where the data tend to cluster) and dispersion (how widely they spread). Together, these measures capture both the “center” and the “stability” of a dataset, forming the foundation of any inferential or computational analysis — from cybersecurity traffic metrics to financial risk assessment.

Measures of Location (Central Tendency)

Measures of location identify the “center” of data. Different averages are used depending on the nature of data and the presence of outliers.

Measure Formula / Definition Typical Use Remarks
Arithmetic Mean Sum of all values / n Symmetric numeric data Most common; sensitive to outliers
Median Middle value in ordered data Skewed or non-normal data Robust to outliers
Mode Most frequent value Categorical or discrete data May be multiple or undefined
Geometric Mean (∏xᵢ)^(1/n) Growth rates, ratios Requires positive data
Harmonic Mean n / Σ(1/xᵢ) Rates and speeds Influenced by small values
Trimmed Mean Mean after removing extremes Data with mild outliers Balance between mean & median

Measures of Dispersion (Variability)

While central tendency locates the typical value, dispersion quantifies the spread. High dispersion implies volatility; low dispersion indicates stability.

Measure Formula / Description Typical Use Notes
Variance Average squared deviation from mean Quantifies spread mathematically Highly sensitive to outliers
Standard Deviation √(Variance) Same units as data Widely used measure of spread
Range Max − Min Quick estimate of spread Completely driven by extremes
Interquartile Range (IQR) Q₃ − Q₁ Middle 50% of data Resistant to outliers
Coefficient of Variation (CV) (σ / μ) Relative variability Useful for comparing across scales

Practical Relevance

In cybersecurity, measures of location and dispersion underpin anomaly detection and behavior modeling. For instance, the mean packet size may describe normal network behavior, while sudden spikes in variance might signal distributed attacks or abnormal data transfers. Hence, these statistics directly translate into operational insight.
