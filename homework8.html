Introduction

This homework deepens the analysis from the previous experiment — the Bernoulli process simulation of the Law of Large Numbers — and investigates its mathematical relationships with binomial coefficients, Pascal’s triangle, the binomial expansion, and other combinatorial structures. Through these connections, the statistical behavior of random cybersecurity events can be interpreted as part of a broader combinatorial and probabilistic framework.

I. Analogy with the Previous Homework

In the earlier homework, each trajectory represented a server’s random walk through \( n \) weeks, with each step modeled as a Bernoulli variable: \[ X_i = \begin{cases} +1, & \text{if secure (probability } 1 - p) \\ -1, & \text{if breached (probability } p) \end{cases} \] The total score after \( n \) steps, \( S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i = 2K - n \), directly depended on the number \( K \) of successful defenses, following a binomial distribution \( K \sim Bin(n, 1 - p) \).

In both homeworks, the Bernoulli process lies at the core: independent trials, binary outcomes, and statistical convergence. In Homework 7, the focus was on the empirical convergence of random walks to a theoretical binomial law; in this homework, the focus shifts to the mathematical and structural properties underlying that same process.

II. The Binomial Framework

The binomial distribution arises from the repeated application of the Bernoulli process. Its probability mass function is given by:

\[ P(K = k) = \binom{n}{k} (1 - p)^k p^{n - k} \]

The coefficients \( \binom{n}{k} \) not only count combinations but also form the entries of Pascal’s triangle, which encodes the recursive identity: \[ \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k} \] This mirrors the recursive, step-by-step nature of both the Bernoulli process and the random walk simulation, where each new observation builds on the previous one.

III. Binomial Expansion and Probabilistic Symmetry

The binomial theorem expands any power of a sum: \[ (a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^k b^{n-k}. \] When \( a = (1 - p) \) and \( b = p \), the entire distribution of possible outcomes for \( n \) trials appears within a single algebraic expression — a compact symbolic version of what the simulation in Homework 7 computed numerically.

As \( n \) grows, the distribution becomes increasingly symmetric around its mean \( E[K] = n(1 - p) \), a reflection of the Law of Large Numbers. This symmetry in the coefficients of the expansion corresponds to the shape of the random walk histogram observed previously.

IV. Fibonacci, Combinatorics, and Recursive Growth

The recursive structure of Pascal’s triangle also appears in the Fibonacci sequence. When summed along its shallow diagonals, Pascal’s triangle generates Fibonacci numbers: \[ F_n = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{n-1-k}{k}. \] This recurrence mirrors the probabilistic evolution of the Bernoulli process, where each new step depends on combinations of earlier possibilities. Such structural overlap illustrates how combinatorial mathematics and probability share the same recursive DNA.

In cybersecurity terms, this can be viewed as the recursive propagation of defense success probabilities through time — each new defense builds on multiple potential past states, exactly like Fibonacci recursion aggregates past outcomes.

V. Discussion and Conclusion

Both Homework 7 and Homework 8 revolve around the same Bernoulli foundation. Homework 7 visualized randomness through simulation and convergence; Homework 8 formalizes that behavior through combinatorial and algebraic structures.

The connection between the binomial coefficients, Pascal’s triangle, the binomial theorem, and even Fibonacci recursion reveals a unifying principle: discrete probability and combinatorics describe the same underlying symmetry. Whether modeled as a cybersecurity random walk or expressed as an algebraic expansion, the mathematics of repetition, accumulation, and convergence remains the same.
